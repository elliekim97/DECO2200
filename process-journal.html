Introduction

Expert consensus is that autonomous cars will be in general regular usage by 2027 with specific use cases live before then (Bainbridge, 2018). Much of the public focus on this new technology has been on autonomous cars replacing the transit tasks undertaken by personally owned and driven cars, human driven taxis and airport shuttles.

Together with my fellow group members Salma and Ben, our team explored the problem area of non-native speaking users of public transport in Sydney. This involved researching immigrants, tourists and international students and how they navigated around the city of Sydney.

Our team aimed to tackle these issues in the development of our concept, leading up to the creation of paper and low-fidelity prototypes. We then collaborated to create a mobile application for the autonomous tourist bus service; the AUTOURBUS.

Week 1: Background Research

In the first lecture of the semester led by Hamish, we were told about the difference between a good and a bad user interface design. It must be a "visible, audible, tactile, gustatory or olfactory point of contact between the user and the computer." The objectives and the context of the design project assignments together align to reveal that we should have a good understanding of a UCD process, prototyping skills, group collaboration and most importantly the interactivity between humans and autonomous vehicles. With the self-driving cars slowly becoming a reality due to the advancements in technology and AI, this idea was quite a new yet old concept to me; because a) I'm sure everyone (at one point of their life) has thought about the possibility of a driverless or flying car operated by robots one day b) Companies like Uber are actually aiming to have flying cars by 2020

However

c) Autonomous cars are still being tested and they can't guarantee 100% safety - there has been fatality incidents in Florida (Tesla in 2016), Arizona (Uber in 2018)

d) Only a small percentage of people in the world are convinced that a robot is a better driver than a human being. People don't fully trust a driverless vehicle yet!

John Preston and Ben Waterson, researcher and lecturer at the University of Southampton states in their article "Transport’s innovation problem: why haven’t flying cars taken off?" (2015) that:
"…transport is not just about technology. It is also about people – and people don’t always like change.".
With this in mind, I posed the following questions which I aim to focus on throughout the assessments:
1. What is the purpose of an autonomous vehicle?
2. What are some existing vehicles in the market that has been successful? What can we learn from them?
3. What level of outcome would users want to achieve from choosing a robot over a human driver?
4. How will I design an innovative interface that displays good GUI and interaction design - by tackling the current issues people have with autonomous vehicles?

Week 5: Concept Development

Our findings from our individual reports were pooled together to be analysed as a whole. Some interesting points that arose were:

  • Driving in Sydney is stressful, and so Public Transport is a better alternative 
  • Due to unfamiliarity, users aren't sure when to get on and off
  • Users are heavily reliant on phone to navigate (as opposed to kiosks and signage)
  • Users are wary of using autonomous vehicles, only if it's a guaranteed fact that it is safe  - Users don't rely on one app - they use a combination of TripView, NextThere or GoogleMaps, depending on what service they're using 
  • Migrants would still rather use English service in order to learn the language 
  • Need for visual and audible stimulation.


From this, we created an objective: Develop an interaction with autonomous vehicles that eases stress and eliminates confusion for non-native speaking tourists navigating around Sydney.This is a statement we will refer back to throughout the design process, to ensure that we will meet the user’s need and wants to create the best possible solution.

Week 7: CONCEPT REFINEMENT

Through refinement of our concept, a Storyboard was created first and foremost - to visualise the experience of the typical user. From this, we decided what tools and features would be required in our interface:

  • A variety of languages that users can change their app to
  • A notification/alarm system to let users know when to get on and off
  • A guided tour guide that users can listen to via their headphones in their preferred language
  • A feature that allows users to browse through current events in Sydney


Week 8: PAPER PROTOTYPE

This week was spent creating sketches of our app. A large number of rough sketches were made, and tested on users so that they can gain a feel of the app. These apps were put through POP. We tested on other students in the Wilkinson building, employing the think-aloud protocol to gain insights to their attitude towards certain features of the app. We gave the user a scenario and asked them to use the app accordingly, and they succeeded in doing so. It was made clear that our concept was running smoothly.


Previous Next

As the users thought-aloud while they were interacting with the paper prototypes, observations were also made. Things we picked up upon were where their fingers hovered towards, or where did their eyes look at first. It was clear that the participants looked for headings and visual cues. It was also made apparent that the navigation bar had too many icons, and that the boxes are too small for them to click. We then thought into account the potential of streamlining the navigation bar, as this will also avoid confusion yet declutter the aesthetic.
